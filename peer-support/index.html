Peer Support

Walking alongside people through crisis, stigma, transition, and systems that can make survival feel harder than it should.

by Raven Indigo Carrigg

LinkedIn Profile Homepage

I am an OHA-certified Peer Support Specialist in Adult Mental Health based in Portland, Oregon. My peer support values grow from lived experience, recovery, neurodivergence, queerness, crisis, grief, trauma, systems navigation, and the long work of learning how to stay human inside conditions that can make a person feel invisible.

Peer support, as I understand it, is not about fixing people, directing their lives, or turning recovery into a performance of wellness. It is about presence, mutuality, dignity, voice, choice, autonomy, and self-determination. It is about helping people reconnect with their own needs, boundaries, inner compass, and reasons to stay.

This page is not an advertisement for services. It is a statement of values: a way of naming what kind of care matters, what kind of support helped me survive, and what it can mean to walk beside someone without asking them to erase themselves.

Voice & choice

People deserve to name their own experience, define their own needs, and participate in decisions that affect their lives.

Dignity & autonomy

Support should reduce shame and overwhelm without taking control away from the person seeking support.

Mutuality & presence

Peer support is grounded in shared humanity, not hierarchy. Sometimes the most important intervention is staying present.

Recovery is not a straight line out of crisis. Sometimes it is a shoreline: return, rupture, tide, breath.

Recovery as lived practice

Recovery is not a fixed destination

I do not understand recovery as the absence of symptoms or the arrival at a permanent state of being “better.” I understand it as the ongoing practice of building agency, safety, connection, and meaning over time.

It can look like learning a nervous system’s warning signs, using DBT skills, asking for help before isolation hardens, resting without shame, naming a boundary, finding practical support, or choosing to stay through a moment that once felt impossible.

My own recovery has been communal, cyclical, and ongoing. Therapy, medication, DBT, affirming care, Oregon Health Plan, NAMI, peer spaces, family support, chosen connection, creativity, and material stability have all mattered. No single breakthrough saved everything. Survival came through a web of support strong enough to hold.

Peer support philosophy

Walking alongside, not ahead

Peer support is not rescue, correction, diagnosis, or control. It is a relationship built through trust, respect, lived experience, and the belief that each person is the expert in their own life.

Listening without replacing the story Support begins by treating a person’s experience as real without forcing it into a narrative that belongs to someone else.
Reducing overwhelm Peer support can help make next steps feel smaller, clearer, and less impossible, especially inside systems that feel confusing or hostile.
Honoring self-determination The goal is not compliance or perfection. The goal is to support people as they reconnect with their own values, choices, and agency.
Staying human in crisis Crisis does not make someone less worthy of dignity. Distress is not a character flaw. Survival does not have to be tidy to be real.

Systems, stigma, and survival

Care should not require erasure

I come to peer support with lived experience navigating healthcare, behavioral health, legal, housing, and bureaucratic systems that can become overwhelming, dismissive, or difficult to trust. Those experiences shape my commitment to accessibility, consent, plain language, and practical support.

Systems can make people feel like their pain is inconvenient, their story is suspect, or their existence is conditional. Peer support can interrupt that by restoring reality, dignity, and choice: “What happened to you matters. What you need matters. You are still a person here.”

I am especially interested in supporting queer, trans, and neurodivergent people, as well as anyone navigating mental health challenges, crisis, stigma, housing instability, healthcare systems, bureaucratic processes, systemic harm, or major life transitions.

Endurance is not the same thing as silence. Survival can become voice, art, boundary, and witness.

Training and grounding

Peer values in practice

I completed peer support training in Adult Mental Health through NAMI Multnomah and am certified through the Oregon Health Authority as a Peer Support Specialist. My training emphasized the role and scope of peer support, recovery and resiliency, communication, cultural humility, trauma-informed practice, self-determination, systems navigation, crisis response, boundaries, confidentiality, and ethical use of lived experience.

Harm reduction Meeting people where they are, reducing danger and shame, and supporting steps that are possible now.
Cultural humility Staying curious, accountable, and aware that every person’s worldview, identity, history, and recovery path are their own.
Trauma-informed practice Supporting safety, choice, collaboration, trust, and empowerment while avoiding unnecessary retraumatization.
Ethical lived experience Using personal history carefully, with boundaries, humility, and respect for the peer’s story rather than centering the supporter.

I have been through fire. My peer support values come from the place inside me that refused to stay silent in a world that too often asks people to make themselves smaller.

I believe no one should have to erase themselves in order to stay alive, be believed, access care, or have their humanity recognized.

Recovery is allowed to be nonlinear. Survival is allowed to be complicated. People are allowed to be works in progress and still belong.
