Systems and Simulations gathers Raven Carrigg’s computational art, complex systems experiments, remote sensing visualizations, and mathematical modeling work.

These projects explore how form emerges from rules, data, feedback, spatial structure, nonlinear interaction, and time. Some begin as models or visualizations; others become moving images, abstract organisms, terrain studies, or records of environmental change.

Across artificial life, cellular automata, digital elevation models, LANDSAT time series, and computational poetics, the throughline is the same: language and number as neighboring abstractions for noticing pattern, tracing transformation, and giving shape to complexity without making it simple.

Artificial life

Lenia and Orbium

Lenia is a continuous cellular automata system for artificial life, developed by Bert Wang-Chak Chan. Orbium is one of its most recognizable self-organizing lifeforms: a small computational organism whose motion and persistence emerge from local update rules.

Reference: Bert Wang-Chak Chan, Lenia: Biology of Artificial Life, 2019.

Read the Lenia paper on arXiv.

Cellular automata

Cyclic cellular automata

Cyclic cellular automata use simple local rules to produce traveling waves, spirals, domains, collisions, and other emergent spatial patterns. These images explore rule-based pattern formation through color, repetition, and nonlinear interaction.

Cyclic cellular automata were developed by David Griffeath. Rule notation and examples here follow the cyclic cellular automata framing described by Softology.

Read Softology’s cyclic cellular automata overview.

Terrain visualization

Digital elevation models

These QGIS-based digital elevation model visualizations translate terrain into color, relief, drainage, and structure. They show landform as pattern: volcanic fields, river islands, channels, ridges, and subtle surface memory.

Remote sensing

LANDSAT time series

These animations use LANDSAT imagery visualized through Sentinel Hub to show environmental and spatial change across decades: glacial retreat, vegetation recovery, river migration, urban expansion, agricultural mosaics, and changing water boundaries.

These time series are intended as visual explorations of landscape change rather than formal remote-sensing classifications. Some use vegetation or water-sensitive spectral treatments; where the exact index is uncertain, captions describe the visible process rather than assigning a specific index label.

Throughline

Language, number, pattern

These projects sit beside Raven’s poetry and peer support work as part of the same underlying practice: listening for structure in complexity without forcing it into a single story.

Computational systems, poems, maps, simulations, and lived-experience work all ask related questions: What forms appear under pressure? How does change become visible? What patterns persist, break, recur, or transform?

For Raven, mathematics and poetry are neighboring forms of abstraction: different ways of shaping attention, tracing emergence, and making meaning at the edge of chaos.
