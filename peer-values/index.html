Peer Values

Walking alongside voice, agency, dignity, and possibility.

by Raven Indigo Carrigg

LinkedIn Profile Homepage

Peer support begins with the belief that people are not problems to be solved. People are living, changing, relational beings whose needs, stories, strengths, and possibilities often become clearer when they are met with dignity, choice, patience, and practical support.

I am an OHA-certified Peer Support Specialist in Adult Mental Health based in Portland, Oregon. My lived experience is central to how I understand this work: not as a script for anyone else’s recovery, but as one doorway into values that are larger than any one story. I understand peer support as a practice of walking alongside people as they reconnect with voice, agency, meaning, resources, and self-determination.

Care often begins before a story becomes clean, linear, or easy to explain. When someone’s experience is fragmented, nonlinear, difficult to name, or too easily dismissed, support can mean making room for reality as it is. From there, support can become practical: clarifying choices, reducing overwhelm, finding resources, navigating systems, and helping someone take the next step without losing themselves in the process.

Voice & choice

People deserve space to name their own experience, define their own needs, and participate in decisions that affect their lives.

Dignity & self-determination

Support should reduce shame and overwhelm while honoring each person’s right to choose their own path.

Meaning & practical support

People need room to make sense of what they have lived through, and support with the material realities that shape daily survival.

Recovery is not always a straight line out of crisis. Sometimes it is reflection, return, support, and small forms of meaning made slowly.

Recovery as lived practice

Recovery is relational and nonlinear

I do not understand recovery as the absence of symptoms or the arrival at a permanent state of being “better.” I understand it as the ongoing practice of building agency, safety, connection, and meaning over time.

Recovery can look like learning a nervous system’s warning signs, using skills that help with distress and emotion regulation, asking for help before isolation hardens, resting without shame, naming a limit, finding practical support, or choosing to stay through a moment that once felt impossible.

It can also look like slowly finding language for what happened when there is no simple plot, no perfect evidence, no single scene that explains everything. Some forms of harm are cumulative, relational, systemic, material, and difficult to narrate. A story does not have to be perfectly linear to be real.

Peer support philosophy

People are not problems to solve

Peer support is not rescue, correction, diagnosis, or control. It is a relationship built through trust, mutuality, respect, lived experience, and the belief that each person is the expert in their own life, including the parts of that life that are still difficult to name.

Walking alongside, not ahead Support means staying with someone as they identify their own needs, choices, values, and next steps.
Listening without replacing the story Support begins by treating a person’s experience as real without forcing it into a narrative that belongs to someone else.
Honoring self-determination The goal is not compliance or perfection. The goal is to support people as they reconnect with their own agency.
Supporting practical next steps Peer support can help reduce overwhelm, identify resources, clarify options, and make difficult systems easier to approach.

Reality, systems, and survival

Making reality visible, then making next steps possible

Many people arrive in support spaces carrying experiences that have been minimized, misunderstood, pathologized, or treated as too complicated to take seriously. Harm may not fit neatly into a form, diagnosis, timeline, or official category. It may live in patterns, silences, power dynamics, material barriers, bureaucratic loops, contradictions, and the ache of not being believed.

That is where language and meaning matter. Before someone can navigate a system, ask for help, or make a next choice, they may need support in recovering their own sense of reality: “What happened to you matters. What you noticed matters. What you need matters. You are still a person here.”

From there, support can become concrete. It can mean sorting through information, preparing for appointments, identifying resources, documenting what matters, translating confusing processes into plain language, or breaking an overwhelming situation into next steps that preserve dignity and choice.

I am especially interested in supporting queer, trans, and neurodivergent people, as well as anyone navigating mental health challenges, crisis, stigma, invisible harm, fractured narratives, systemic harm, healthcare systems, housing instability, bureaucratic overwhelm, or major life transitions.

Endurance is not the same thing as silence. Survival can become voice, limit, action, and witness.

Meaning, context, and practical support

People are shaped by more than one story

Across my writing, systems thinking, and peer values, I am interested in how people make meaning inside conditions that are complex, relational, and changing. People are shaped by memory, body, community, material conditions, language, power, and time. Peer support can help create conditions where voice, agency, connection, and next steps become easier to access.

Language can restore agency Finding words for an experience can help a person locate themselves again after being blurred, dismissed, or overwritten.
Patterns can hold what timelines cannot Complex experiences do not always make sense as a single sequence of events. Sometimes the pattern is part of what needs to be understood.
Meaning does not have to be tidy A story can be unfinished, conflicted, nonlinear, and still worthy of care, action, support, and change.
Repair includes material reality Meaning-making matters, and so do housing, food, healthcare, safety, transportation, paperwork, money, rest, and community.

Self-abandonment, service, and dignity

Support should not ask people to disappear

Some people learn early that their value comes from managing other people’s emotions, keeping the peace, being useful, staying agreeable, or carrying more than one person should have to carry. Peer support can help interrupt that pattern by honoring the person underneath the role: not just helper, survivor, caretaker, client, patient, problem, or case.

Needs are not failures Having needs does not make someone selfish, broken, demanding, or too much.
Limits protect aliveness Naming a limit can be part of reconnecting with the self, not a rejection of care or connection.
People are more than survival roles No one should have to be useful, easy, healed, or legible to deserve dignity.
Belonging is not earned by erasure Support should help people take up space in their own lives, not become smaller to stay acceptable.

Training and grounding

Peer values in practice

I completed peer support training in Adult Mental Health through NAMI Multnomah and am certified through the Oregon Health Authority as a Peer Support Specialist. My training emphasized the role and scope of peer support, recovery and resiliency, communication, cultural humility, trauma-informed practice, self-determination, systems navigation, crisis response, confidentiality, and ethical use of lived experience.

Harm reduction Meeting people where they are, reducing danger and shame, and supporting steps that are possible now.
Cultural humility Staying curious, accountable, and aware that every person’s worldview, identity, history, and recovery path are their own.
Trauma-informed practice Supporting safety, choice, collaboration, trust, and empowerment while reducing the risk of retraumatization.
Ethical lived experience Using personal history carefully, with scope, humility, and respect for the peer’s story rather than centering the supporter.

People are not fixed problems. People change in relationship with their bodies, histories, communities, environments, choices, and material conditions.

I believe no one should have to erase themselves in order to stay alive, be believed, access care, or have their humanity recognized.

Recovery is allowed to be nonlinear. Survival is allowed to be complicated. People are allowed to be works in progress and still belong.
