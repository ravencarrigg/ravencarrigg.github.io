Raven's Peer Values

Making room for voice, dignity, agency, and self-determined change.

by Raven Indigo Carrigg

Peer support begins with the belief that people are not problems to be solved. People are whole, changing human beings whose needs, strengths, stories, and choices deserve dignity.

I am an OHA-certified Peer Support Specialist in Adult Mental Health and a systems navigator based in Portland, Oregon. I come to peer support from a life shaped by neurodivergence, queerness, transness, grief, crisis, recovery, complex trauma, and repeated experiences navigating systems that can be overwhelming, confusing, dismissive, harmful, or hostile.

My lived experience is central to how I understand this work: not as a script for anyone else’s recovery, but as one doorway into peer values that are larger than any one story. I understand peer support as walking alongside people rather than trying to fix them.

Voice, choice, and dignity

People are not problems to solve

Peer support is not rescue, correction, diagnosis, or control. It is a relationship built through trust, mutuality, respect, collaboration, and lived experience.

Support means listening without judgment, treating people’s experiences as real, and not trying to fit them into stories that are not theirs. It means believing people when they name what they have lived through and respecting their pace as they identify their own needs, choices, values, resources, and next steps.

The goal is not perfection. The goal is to make agency more accessible while honoring autonomy, dignity, and self-determination by giving people a seat at their own table.

Recovery as lived practice

Recovery is cyclical and nonlinear

I do not believe recovery is about correcting what is “wrong.” I believe recovery is about finding your voice again, staying connected to what matters to you, and remembering that you belong, even when the world makes that hard.

Recovery can include support, skills, practical resources, community, and the slow work of finding language for what happened, what matters now, and what may need time, care, and room to change.

My own recovery has been communal, cyclical, and ongoing. I bring that lived experience into this work by meeting people where they are and walking beside them, not ahead of them.

Systems navigation and practical support

People are shaped by more than one story

People do not move through crisis in a vacuum. Housing, healthcare, benefits, transportation, legal or bureaucratic processes, money, safety, rest, relationships, and community can all shape what recovery makes possible.

Sometimes peer support looks like quiet, nonjudgmental presence. Sometimes it looks like practical support: helping reduce overwhelm, organize information, clarify options, prepare for appointments, document what matters, navigate housing or healthcare, or make a difficult system easier to approach.

Alongside peer support, I bring an interdisciplinary background in applied mathematics, computational modeling, research, documentation, writing, teaching, advocacy, and hands-on systems building. I am drawn to complexity, whether that means noticing patterns, making systems more accessible, or helping someone feel less alone inside a difficult process.

My work is grounded in harm reduction, cultural humility, voice and choice, and respect for autonomy and self-determination. My training through NAMI Multnomah and certification through the Oregon Health Authority further ground this work in recovery and resiliency, trauma-informed practice, confidentiality, systems navigation, crisis response, and ethical use of lived experience.

I am open to supporting anyone navigating mental health challenges, crisis, stigma, systemic harm, healthcare systems, housing instability, legal or bureaucratic processes, or major life transitions. Because of my own lived experience, I may be especially well suited to supporting queer, trans, and neurodivergent people, but my commitment to dignity, agency, and access to care is not limited to any one community.

No one should have to disappear inside the systems they are trying to survive, or erase themselves in order to stay alive, be believed, access care, or have their humanity recognized.
