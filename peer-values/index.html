Peer support begins with the belief that people are not problems to be solved. People are whole, changing human beings whose needs, strengths, stories, and choices deserve dignity.

I am an OHA-certified Peer Support Specialist in Adult Mental Health and a systems navigator based in Portland, Oregon. I come to peer support from a life shaped by neurodivergence, queerness, transness, grief, crisis, recovery, complex trauma, and repeated experiences navigating systems that can be overwhelming, confusing, dismissive, harmful, or hostile.

My lived experience is central to how I understand this work: not as a script for anyone else’s recovery, but as one doorway into peer values that are larger than any one story. I know how much it matters when someone listens without judgment, treats your experience as real, and does not try to fit you into a story that is not yours.

I understand peer support as walking alongside people as they reconnect with voice, agency, meaning, resources, and self-determination, especially when crisis, stigma, trauma, or systems have made those things harder to access.

Voice, choice, and dignity

People are not problems to solve

Peer support is not rescue, correction, diagnosis, or control. It is a relationship built through trust, mutuality, respect, and lived experience.

Support means staying with someone as they identify their own needs, choices, values, resources, and next steps. The goal is not perfection. The goal is to make agency more accessible while honoring each person’s right to choose their own path.

For me, this means believing people when they name what they have lived through, respecting their pace, and honoring autonomy, dignity, and self-determination by giving people a seat at their own table.

Recovery as lived practice

Recovery is cyclical and nonlinear

I do not believe recovery is about correcting what is “wrong.” I understand recovery as an ongoing process of building safety, connection, agency, and meaning over time.

Recovery can include skills, rest, limits, support, practical resources, community, and the slow work of finding language for experiences that do not fit a simple timeline. It can move in cycles, returns, setbacks, and renewed choices.

My own recovery has been communal, cyclical, and ongoing. I bring that lived experience into this work by meeting people where they are and walking beside them, not ahead of them.

Meaning, systems, and material reality

People are shaped by more than one story

People are shaped by body, memory, community, culture, power, environment, relationships, and material conditions. Support has to make room for that complexity without reducing a person to paperwork, symptoms, or a single story.

Meaning-making matters, and so do housing, healthcare, benefits, safety, transportation, legal or bureaucratic processes, money, rest, and community. Sometimes peer support looks like quiet presence. Sometimes it looks like helping reduce overwhelm, organize information, clarify options, document what matters, or make a difficult system easier to approach.

I am drawn to complexity, whether that means noticing patterns, making systems more accessible, or helping someone feel less alone inside a difficult process.

My training through NAMI Multnomah and certification through the Oregon Health Authority ground this work in recovery and resiliency, harm reduction, cultural humility, trauma-informed practice, self-determination, confidentiality, systems navigation, crisis response, and ethical use of lived experience.

I am especially interested in supporting queer, trans, and neurodivergent people, as well as people navigating mental health challenges, crisis, stigma, systemic harm, healthcare systems, housing instability, legal or bureaucratic processes, or major life transitions.

I do this work from the part of me that knows no one should have to erase themselves in order to stay alive, be believed, access care, or have their humanity recognized.

People are allowed to be works in progress and still belong.
