Peer Values

Walking alongside voice, agency, dignity, and possibility.

by Raven Indigo Carrigg

LinkedIn Profile Homepage

Peer support begins with the belief that people are not problems to be solved. People are living, changing, relational beings whose needs, stories, strengths, and possibilities often become clearer when they are met with dignity, choice, patience, and practical support.

I am an OHA-certified Peer Support Specialist in Adult Mental Health based in Portland, Oregon. My lived experience is central to how I understand this work: not as a script for anyone else’s recovery, but as one doorway into values that are larger than any one story. I understand peer support as a practice of walking alongside people as they reconnect with voice, agency, meaning, resources, and self-determination.

For me, peer support is grounded in mutuality, harm reduction, cultural humility, trauma-informed practice, and respect for each person’s right to choose their own path.

Peer support philosophy

People are not problems to solve

Peer support is not rescue, correction, diagnosis, or control. It is a relationship built through trust, respect, lived experience, and the belief that each person is the expert in their own life.

Support means walking alongside someone as they identify their own needs, choices, values, resources, and next steps. The goal is not compliance or perfection. The goal is to help make agency more accessible, especially when shame, crisis, overwhelm, stigma, or systems have made a person feel reduced to a problem.

  • Voice and choice: people deserve space to name their own experience and participate in decisions that affect their lives.
  • Dignity and self-determination: support should reduce shame without taking control away from the person seeking support.
  • Meaning and practical support: people need room to make sense of what they have lived through, and support with the material realities that shape daily survival.

Recovery is not always a straight line out of crisis.

Recovery as lived practice

Recovery is relational and nonlinear

I do not understand recovery as the absence of symptoms or the arrival at a permanent state of being “better.” I understand it as the ongoing practice of building agency, safety, connection, and meaning over time.

Recovery can look like learning a nervous system’s warning signs, using skills that help with distress and emotion regulation, asking for help before isolation hardens, resting without shame, naming a limit, finding practical support, or choosing to stay through a moment that once felt impossible.

It can also mean finding language for what happened when there is no simple plot, no perfect evidence, and no single scene that explains everything. A story does not have to be perfectly linear to be real.

Reality, systems, and survival

Making reality visible, then making next steps possible

Many people arrive in support spaces carrying experiences that have been minimized, misunderstood, pathologized, or treated as too complicated to take seriously.

Harm may not fit neatly into a form, diagnosis, timeline, or official category. It may live in patterns, silences, power dynamics, material barriers, bureaucratic loops, contradictions, and the ache of not being believed.

Before someone can navigate a system, ask for help, or make a next choice, they may need support in recovering their own sense of reality: what happened matters, what they noticed matters, what they need matters, and they are still a person here.

From there, support can become concrete: sorting information, preparing for appointments, identifying resources, documenting what matters, translating confusing processes into plain language, or breaking an overwhelming situation into next steps that preserve dignity and choice.

Survival can become voice, limit, action, and witness.

Meaning, context, and practical support

People are shaped by more than one story

Across my writing, systems thinking, and peer values, I am interested in how people make meaning inside conditions that are complex, relational, and changing.

People are shaped by memory, body, community, material conditions, language, power, and time. Peer support can help create conditions where voice, agency, connection, and next steps become easier to access.

Meaning-making matters, and so do housing, food, healthcare, safety, transportation, paperwork, money, rest, and community. Support should be able to hold both: the story a person is trying to understand and the practical realities they are trying to survive.

Self-abandonment, service, and dignity

Support should not ask people to disappear

Some people learn early that their value comes from managing other people’s emotions, keeping the peace, being useful, staying agreeable, or carrying more than one person should have to carry.

Peer support can help interrupt that pattern by honoring the person underneath the role: not just helper, survivor, caretaker, client, patient, problem, or case.

Needs are not failures. Limits can protect aliveness. Belonging should not require erasure. No one should have to be useful, easy, healed, or perfectly legible to deserve dignity.

Training and grounding

Peer values in practice

I completed peer support training in Adult Mental Health through NAMI Multnomah and am certified through the Oregon Health Authority as a Peer Support Specialist.

My training emphasized the role and scope of peer support, recovery and resiliency, communication, cultural humility, trauma-informed practice, self-determination, systems navigation, crisis response, confidentiality, and ethical use of lived experience.

I am especially interested in supporting queer, trans, and neurodivergent people, as well as people navigating mental health challenges, crisis, stigma, invisible or nonlinear experiences, systemic harm, healthcare systems, housing instability, bureaucratic overwhelm, or major life transitions.

People are not fixed problems. People change in relationship with their bodies, histories, communities, environments, choices, and material conditions.

I believe no one should have to erase themselves in order to stay alive, be believed, access care, or have their humanity recognized.

Recovery is allowed to be nonlinear. Survival is allowed to be complicated. People are allowed to be works in progress and still belong.
